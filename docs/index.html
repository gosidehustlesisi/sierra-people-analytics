Figure Gallery

16 analysis outputs from production notebooks — click any image to view full size

💡 16.1% company-wide attrition rate.About 1 in 6 employees in the IBM dataset left voluntarily. The baseline rate sets the bar for prediction accuracy.
💡 Attrition peaks in the 26–35 age group.Younger employees with less tenure show higher departure rates. Gender and department both influence the pattern.
💡 Overtime is the #1 job-level risk factor.Employees who work overtime are dramatically more likely to leave. Travel frequency and job level also matter.
💡 High earners with low satisfaction are highest risk.Income alone doesn't predict retention — the satisfaction-income interaction is the key signal.
💡 Feature correlation reveals redundancy.Years at company, years in role, and total working years are highly correlated — model selection must avoid multicollinearity.
💡 Random Forest ranks overtime, income, and age top 3.The ensemble model confirms what EDA suggested: job conditions matter more than demographics for attrition risk.
💡 87% accuracy with strong ROC-AUC.Model separates leavers from stayers cleanly. Confusion matrix shows low false-negative rate — the model catches most at-risk employees.
💡 Tenure curve shows early-career vulnerability.Attrition is highest in years 0–2, drops through year 5, then stabilizes. Career stage is a stronger predictor than age.
💡 Executive summary: 5 factors drive 80% of attrition.Overtime, income, age, job satisfaction, and years at company capture most predictive signal. Focus here first.
💡 Representation gaps widen at senior levels.Entry-level gender balance is closer to parity. Director and VP levels show the largest gaps — the pipeline is leaking upward.
💡 Oaxaca-Blinder decomposition isolates unexplained gaps.After controlling for role, tenure, and education, 8–12% of pay gap remains unexplained — the equity target for intervention.
💡 Attrition is not uniform across demographic groups.Some groups leave at 2x the rate of others. Retention is both a DEI metric and a business cost.
💡 Promotion rates diverge after year 3.Early career promotion parity is strong, but divergence accelerates at mid-career. The year-3 inflection is where intervention matters most.
💡 Work-life balance scores vary by family status.Employees with dependents score lower on work-life satisfaction. This drives retention risk for a specific demographic segment.
💡 Education pipeline diversity predicts hiring diversity.Programs that recruit from diverse educational backgrounds produce more diverse hires. The pipeline starts before the job posting.
💡 One-page scorecard for board and executive reviews.Representation, pay equity, promotion parity, and retention — all in a single view. Red/yellow/green traffic light system for instant readability.
